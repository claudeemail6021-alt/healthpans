## The Uncomfortable Truth About Your Non-Stick Pan

Most people don't think twice about their cookware. But if you own traditional non-stick pans — especially ones that are more than 3–4 years old — they may be exposing you to chemicals you'd rather avoid.

Here are seven signs it's time to make a change.

## 1. The Coating Is Scratched or Chipped

This is the most urgent warning sign. PTFE (Teflon) coatings are designed to remain intact. When they're scratched — from metal utensils, rough cleaning, or stacking — the coating begins to break down and can flake into your food.

Ingesting small PTFE particles isn't considered acutely dangerous (the particles are largely inert and pass through the body), but it indicates the coating is degrading — and a degrading coating releases more chemical compounds into food and air over time.

**What to do:** If you can see scratches, chips, or any areas where the coating has lifted away from the metal, replace the pan immediately.

## 2. The Pan Is Discolored or Stained

Darkening, staining, or uneven coloration on a non-stick surface is a sign of coating degradation. A healthy non-stick coating should be consistently colored and smooth.

## 3. Food Starts Sticking to a "Non-Stick" Pan

Non-stick coatings degrade over time. If food that used to slide off freely is now sticking, the coating has worn down significantly — which means it's been going somewhere. Either into the air or into your food.

## 4. The Pan Was Made Before 2013

PFOA (perfluorooctanoic acid) — one of the most harmful PFAS compounds — was used to manufacture PTFE coatings until it was phased out around 2013. Cookware from before this date almost certainly contains PFOA residues.

Even if the coating appears intact, older pans carry legacy contamination that makes replacement worth considering.

## 5. You're Cooking at Very High Heat

PTFE coatings are stable up to approximately 500°F (260°C). Above that temperature, they begin to break down and release toxic fluorinated gases. At 570°F (300°C), the degradation accelerates significantly.

If you regularly sear meat, cook stir-fries at high heat, or accidentally overheat an empty pan, you've almost certainly exceeded these thresholds. An overheated PTFE pan can release gases that cause flu-like symptoms (polymer fume fever) in humans — and are known to be fatal to pet birds, whose respiratory systems are far more sensitive.

## 6. You're Cooking for Infants, Pregnant Women, or Immunocompromised People

The people most vulnerable to PFAS exposure are those with developing or compromised systems. If you're cooking for a pregnant person, infant, young child, or someone with a compromised immune system, the precautionary principle applies even more strongly.

PFAS have been linked to disrupted fetal development, reduced vaccine efficacy in children, thyroid disruption, and reduced fertility. The dose makes the poison — but for vulnerable populations, reducing exposure from every controllable source makes sense.

## 7. The Pan Came From a Discount Store Without Brand Information

Not all cheap cookware is dangerous, but anonymous, unbranded non-stick pans — particularly those manufactured without safety oversight — are more likely to contain older PFAS formulations or other toxic compounds like lead and cadmium in their coatings.

If you can't identify the manufacturer or find any information about the coating materials, the safe assumption is to replace it.

## What to Replace It With

If you've identified one or more of these warning signs, here are your best safe alternatives:

**For everyday cooking (eggs, vegetables, gentle heat):**
→ A ceramic-coated pan from GreenPan or Caraway. PFAS-free, easy to use, minimal learning curve.

**For high-heat cooking (searing, browning, stir-fry):**
→ A Lodge cast iron skillet or a carbon steel pan from Made In. No coatings at all — just iron.

**For sauces, soups, and boiling:**
→ An All-Clad or Made In stainless steel saucepan. Fully inert and virtually indestructible.

The transition takes a few weeks of adjustment. But cooking in equipment you trust — knowing it isn't adding chemical exposure to every meal — is worth it.
