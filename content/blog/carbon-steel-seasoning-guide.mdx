## Why Carbon Steel Deserves a Spot in Your Kitchen

Carbon steel is what professional chefs reach for when they want the best of both worlds: the heat retention and non-stick potential of cast iron, with the lighter weight and faster response of stainless steel. It's PFAS-free by nature (no coatings — just iron and carbon), and when properly seasoned, it becomes one of the most capable cooking surfaces in existence.

The catch? It requires a bit of initial work. This guide walks you through everything.

## What Is Carbon Steel?

Carbon steel is an alloy of iron and carbon (typically 1% carbon, 99% iron). It's lighter than cast iron (roughly 30–50% lighter), responds faster to heat changes, and can be shaped thinner while retaining strength.

Like cast iron, carbon steel develops its non-stick properties through **seasoning** — layers of polymerized oil that bond to the metal surface and create a slick, protective coating.

## Before You Start: Strip the Factory Coating

Most new carbon steel pans come with a protective coating (usually beeswax or a thin factory oil) to prevent rust during shipping. This must be removed before seasoning.

**How to strip the coating:**
1. Scrub the pan with warm, soapy water and a brush or steel wool
2. Rinse thoroughly
3. Dry completely — either with a towel and then on the stove over medium heat for 2–3 minutes
4. The bare metal will look dull gray. This is correct.

## Step-by-Step Seasoning

You'll need: a high smoke-point oil (flaxseed, grapeseed, or refined avocado oil), paper towels, and an oven or stovetop.

### Oven Method (Recommended for First Seasoning)

1. **Preheat your oven to 450–500°F (230–260°C)**
2. **Apply a very thin layer of oil** to the entire pan — inside, outside, and handle — using a paper towel. Then wipe off as much as you can. The pan should look almost dry, not greasy. Too much oil = sticky, gummy surface.
3. **Place upside-down in the oven** for 1 hour. The upside-down position lets excess oil drip off rather than pool.
4. **Let it cool in the oven** for 30+ minutes.
5. **Repeat 3–5 times.** Each layer builds on the last. More layers = better seasoning.

### Stovetop Method (Quick Maintenance)

1. Heat the pan over medium heat until smoking slightly
2. Add a tiny amount of oil (1/2 teaspoon or less)
3. Rub it across the surface with a paper towel, using tongs to protect your hand
4. Heat until the oil stops smoking
5. Remove from heat and let cool

## The First Few Cooks

Treat your newly seasoned carbon steel pan gently for the first 5–10 cooks. This helps build up the seasoning layers.

**Start with high-fat foods:** bacon, sautéed vegetables with butter, pan-fried potatoes. These add fat to the surface and reinforce the seasoning.

**Avoid for the first month:**
- Acidic foods (tomatoes, citrus, wine-based sauces)
- Wet or braised foods that sit in the pan for a long time
- Dishwasher (ever)

## Ongoing Care

**After each use:**
1. While still warm, wipe out food residue with a paper towel
2. If food is stuck, add a splash of water and simmer briefly, then wipe out
3. Rinse with hot water if needed — soap occasionally in small amounts is fine, but don't make it a habit
4. Dry immediately and thoroughly (wet carbon steel rusts fast)
5. Apply a very thin coat of oil before storing

## Troubleshooting

**Sticky surface?** You used too much oil when seasoning. Strip it back with steel wool and start over.

**Rust spots?** Normal with neglect. Scrub with steel wool until rust is gone, re-dry, and re-season.

**Food sticking?** Your seasoning isn't built up enough yet, or you're not preheating long enough. Carbon steel needs 2–3 minutes of preheating before adding food.

**Patchy black and gray?** This is normal during early seasoning. With time, the surface will darken to a consistent black.
