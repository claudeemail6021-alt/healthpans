## What Are PFAS — And Why Should You Care?

PFAS stands for **per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances** — a group of more than 12,000 synthetic chemicals that have been used in manufacturing since the 1940s. You've probably heard them called "forever chemicals," and the nickname is apt: PFAS don't break down in the environment or in the human body.

They've been found in drinking water, soil, wildlife, and yes — your cookware.

### The Cookware Connection

The most well-known PFAS in cookware is **PTFE** (polytetrafluoroethylene), better known by DuPont's brand name Teflon. PTFE itself is considered relatively inert at normal cooking temperatures. The problem is what it's made with — or what it breaks down into.

- **PFOA** (perfluorooctanoic acid) was used to manufacture PTFE coatings until it was phased out around 2013. PFOA is a confirmed carcinogen linked to kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, and ulcerative colitis.
- **GenX chemicals** replaced PFOA — but emerging research suggests they carry similar risks.
- At temperatures above **500°F (260°C)**, PTFE coatings begin to degrade and release toxic fluorinated gases. Polymer fume fever is a real, documented condition.

## How to Identify PFAS-Free Cookware

Reading cookware labels requires some detective work. Here's what to look for:

### Green flags ✅
- "PFAS-free" with third-party certification
- "PTFE-free" (means no Teflon-type coatings at all)
- Materials with no synthetic coatings: cast iron, carbon steel, stainless steel, enameled cast iron

### Red flags 🚩
- "PFOA-free" only (PTFE may still be present, along with other PFAS)
- Vague claims like "eco-friendly coating" without specifics
- Generic "non-stick" without material disclosure

### Ceramic: A Note of Caution

Many "ceramic" pans are marketed as non-toxic, and most legitimate ceramic coatings (sol-gel based) are indeed PFAS-free. However, the word "ceramic" is not regulated — some coatings labeled "ceramic" still contain synthetic compounds. Look for brands that explicitly state their sol-gel ceramic formula is free from PFAS, PTFE, lead, and cadmium.

## Transitioning Your Kitchen

You don't have to replace everything at once. Here's a practical phased approach:

**Phase 1 — Highest priority:** Replace your most-used non-stick pan first. This is typically where the most PFAS exposure occurs, especially if the coating is scratched or worn.

**Phase 2 — Medium priority:** Replace other non-stick items (baking sheets, skillets) over the next 6–12 months as budget allows.

**Phase 3 — Optional:** Evaluate specialty pans (woks, griddles) and decide if the exposure risk warrants replacement.

## Bottom Line

The science on PFAS is clear enough that taking a precautionary approach makes sense — especially for cookware you use daily. The good news is that cast iron, carbon steel, and stainless steel alternatives are not only safer but often outperform non-stick in the long run. The learning curve is real, but the payoff — cookware that lasts generations and never leaches chemicals — is worth it.
