Switching to an induction cooktop is one of the best things you can do for indoor air quality — induction produces no combustion gases and heats faster and more precisely than gas or electric coil. But induction has a hard requirement that gas and electric don't: the pan must contain magnetic material to work. This eliminates a surprising number of popular non-toxic cookware options and makes material selection more specific.

## Why Induction Compatibility Matters

Induction cooktops work by creating a magnetic field that directly heats ferromagnetic material in the pan — the cooktop surface itself stays cool. This requires the cookware to contain iron or magnetic stainless steel. Materials that are NOT induction compatible: pure aluminum (no iron), copper (no iron), and most single-ply stainless (non-magnetic austenitic grades).

The simplest compatibility test: hold a refrigerator magnet to the bottom of the pan. If it sticks, the pan will work on induction.

## Non-Toxic Materials That Work on Induction

### Cast Iron — Full Compatibility
All cast iron is induction compatible — pure iron is the original ferromagnetic material. Lodge, Field Company, Staub, Le Creuset (enameled cast iron) all work perfectly. Cast iron's thick base also pairs particularly well with induction's precise heat control — the thermal mass smooths out any minor temperature fluctuations.

**Best for:** Searing, frying, oven use, braising. The best all-around non-toxic induction material.

### Carbon Steel — Full Compatibility
Carbon steel (98-99% iron) is induction compatible and heats faster than cast iron on induction due to its thinner gauge. Many professional cooks prefer carbon steel specifically on induction for this reason — you get responsive temperature control with cast iron's non-toxic credentials.

**Best for:** Stovetop cooking, sautéing, crepes, quick tasks requiring responsive heat. Lighter than cast iron for the same cooking performance.

### Magnetic Stainless Steel — Compatible (Check Specs)
Here's where it gets nuanced. Some stainless steel is magnetic (ferritic and martensitic grades, like 430 stainless) and induction compatible. Some is not (austenitic grades, like the common 304/18-8 stainless). Multi-ply stainless pans from quality brands handle this by using an induction-compatible base layer — All-Clad's D3 uses 18/10 cooking surface with a magnetic stainless base. Made In Stainless is similarly constructed. When buying multi-ply stainless for induction, verify the brand explicitly states induction compatibility — don't assume.

**Best for:** Sauce-making, braising, searing, stovetop-to-oven. Most premium multi-ply stainless is induction compatible by design.

### Enameled Cast Iron — Full Compatibility
Le Creuset, Staub, and Lodge enameled cast iron all work on induction — the iron core is ferromagnetic regardless of the enamel exterior. The enameled surface adds non-reactive protection for acidic foods while maintaining full induction function.

**Best for:** Dutch ovens, braises, soups, slow cooking. The most versatile non-toxic induction option for larger-format cooking.

### Ceramic Non-Stick — Varies by Brand
This is the most confusing category. Standard ceramic non-stick pans use aluminum bases — not induction compatible. Some brands specifically manufacture induction-compatible ceramic versions by adding a magnetic base layer. GreenPan's Valencia Pro line uses a "Magneto" induction base; standard GreenPan Paris Pro does not work on induction. Caraway's standard pans are NOT induction compatible. Always verify before purchasing.

**The workaround:** An induction interface disk (a flat iron disk placed between the cooktop and any non-magnetic pan) allows aluminum-based pans to work on induction — but you lose much of the precision and speed benefit, and it adds another object to manage.

## What to Avoid

**Caraway on induction:** Standard Caraway is not induction compatible — a common and expensive mistake. Verify compatibility before any ceramic purchase.

**Cheap "induction compatible" claims:** Some budget brands add a thin magnetic disk to otherwise non-magnetic pans. These work technically but couple poorly with induction (inefficient heating, uneven heat distribution). Stick to brands where induction compatibility is engineered into the core construction.

**Ceramic + induction adapter disk:** This combination negates most of induction's precision and speed advantages. If you want the benefits of induction, use pans designed for it.

## Adapting Your Existing Cookware

If you're transitioning to induction and have non-compatible pans you love, an induction interface disk ($20–40) allows limited use of aluminum or copper pans. The experience is meaningfully inferior to properly compatible pans — slower heating, less precise control — but it's useful for transition periods or for specialized pans (a beloved copper saucier, for example) where replacement isn't practical.
